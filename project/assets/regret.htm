Formalizing Online Learning Regret Bounds in Lean 4

We derive a rigorous upper bound on the regret of an online learner. Unlike batch optimization, we make no assumptions about the distribution of future data. Instead, we establish a foundational setup where the learner selects iterates from a convex set $\mathcal{K}$ and suffers losses from an arbitrary sequence of convex, Lipschitz functions $f_t$.

The following derivation establishes the global $O(\sqrt{T})$ rate by first analyzing the geometric stability of a single step and then accumulating these local bounds via induction.

Definition: First-Order Convexity Inequality

Claim:

$$f(y) \ge f(x) + ⟪g, y - x⟫$$

Notation:

  • $x$ : The current iterate where the subgradient is evaluated.
  • $y$ : An arbitrary point in the domain (the comparator $u$ in later proofs).
  • $g$ : The subgradient vector, acting as a linear lower-bound for the function.
  • $y - x$ : The displacement vector from the current iterate to the target.
  • $⟪\cdot, \cdot⟫$ : The standard inner product, measuring directional alignment.

Goal:

Convexity is the "engine" of optimization. This specific formulation linearizes the function, replacing a complex global gap $f(y) - f(x)$ with a simple geometric interaction. This allows Lean to work with structural inner product properties rather than arbitrary function curves.

Full Narrative

Convexity is the fundamental structural assumption that makes online learning tractable. At its core, it says: a function lies above all of its tangent lines. For a differentiable convex function $f$, at any point $x$ with gradient $g = \nabla f(x)$, we have:

$$f(y) \ge f(x) + \langle g, y - x \rangle \quad \forall y.$$

This inequality is remarkable because it linearizes the function: instead of reasoning about the full global shape of $f$, we only need the local gradient $g$ and a simple inner product. The term $\langle g, y - x \rangle$ captures how much the function could change if we move from $x$ to $y$, under the best possible (linear) approximation.

In our OGD proof, this lemma will let us replace the true objective gap $f_t(x_t) - f_t(u)$ — which we cannot directly control — with the inner product $\langle g_t, x_t - u \rangle$, which we can control through the geometry of the update step. This is the "linearization reduction" described in §7.3–7.4 of the theory notes.

Key insight: Convexity hands us a free linear lower bound at every point. The entire OGD analysis flows from this single inequality.

def convex_function_first_order_inequality (f : E → ℝ) (g : E) (x : E) : Prop :=
  ∀ y : E, f y ≥ f x + ⟪g, y - x⟫
Definition: Lipschitz Continuity Assumption

Claim:

$$\|g\| \le L$$

Notation:

  • $\|g\|$ : The magnitude or norm of the subgradient.
  • $L$ : A universal speed limit for the function's rate of change.

Goal:

Without this assumption, an adversary could choose a loss function with an infinitely steep gradient, catapulting the learner's state infinitely far away. This bounding property ensures that each discrete "jump" has a finite impact.

Full Narrative

Lipschitz continuity is the second pillar of the OGD proof. It constrains how fast the loss functions can change: for any two points $w, w'$ in the domain, $|f(w) - f(w')| \le L \|w - w'\|$. For differentiable convex functions, this is equivalent to bounding the gradient norm: $\|\nabla f(w)\| \le L$ for all $w$.

Why is this essential? In OGD, the update step moves a distance $\eta \|g_t\|$ before projection. If gradients could be arbitrarily large, a single adversarial loss could throw the learner's iterate outside any bounded region, making the distance-tracking argument impossible.

The Lipschitz constant $L$ appears twice in the final bound $DL\sqrt{T}$: once through the noise term $\eta^2 \|g_t\|^2$ in the squared distance expansion, and once implicitly through the optimal step size $\eta = D/(L\sqrt{T})$. It sets the scale at which the learner must balance exploration (stepping far) and stability (staying close to the comparator).

def lipschitz_continuous_def (g : E) (L : ℝ) : Prop :=
  ‖g‖ ≤ L
Definition: OGD Update Rule

Claim:

$$x_{t+1} = \Pi_{\mathcal{K}}(x_t - \eta_t g_t)$$

Notation:

  • $\eta$ : The learning rate or step size.
  • $\Pi_{\mathcal{K}}$ : The projection onto the feasible convex set.

Goal:

This rule defines the learner's trajectory. By moving against the gradient and projecting back to the set, we ensure the learner's decisions remain valid while aggressively seeking the minimum.

Full Narrative

The OGD update is remarkably simple: take a step opposite the current gradient, then project back onto the feasible set. Despite its simplicity, it is the Euclidean special case of a much broader family — Mirror Descent — as shown in §11.3 of the theory notes.

The projection step $\Pi_{\mathcal{K}}$ is critical: without it, the iterate could drift outside $\mathcal{K}$, violating the problem constraints. The non-expansiveness of convex projections (Lemma 5) ensures that this correction never harms convergence: $\|\Pi_{\mathcal{K}}(v) - \Pi_{\mathcal{K}}(u)\| \le \|v - u\|$.

In the dual-averaging perspective (§10.1), OGD maintains a running sum of gradients in the dual space and maps back to the primal via the inverse of $\nabla \psi$ where $\psi(w) = \frac{1}{2}\|w\|_2^2$. The recursive form $x_{t+1} = x_t - \eta g_t$ (before projection) falls out because $\nabla \psi$ and its inverse are both the identity for the Euclidean potential.

Lean 4 Code 
def ogd_update_rule_def (P : Projection E) (η : ℝ) (x g : E) : E :=
  P.proj (x - η • g)
Lemma: Squared Distance Expansion

Claim:

$$\text{dist}(x_{t+1}, u)^2 \le \text{dist}(x_t, u)^2 - 2\eta\langle g_t, x_t - u \rangle + \eta^2 \|g_t\|^2$$

Goal:

This reveals the fundamental tension in OGD: the alignment term shrinks our distance to the goal, while the noise term expands it as a penalty for the discrete step size.

Full Narrative

This is the workhorse lemma of the entire OGD proof. It relates the change in distance to the comparator $u$ across one OGD step to two quantities: the alignment $\langle g_t, x_t - u\rangle$ (how well the gradient points toward $u$) and the squared gradient norm $\|g_t\|^2$ (a penalty for moving at all).

The derivation proceeds in two stages. First, by the non-expansiveness of projection (Lemma 5), $\text{dist}(x_{t+1}, u) \le \text{dist}(x_t - \eta g_t, u)$. Squaring both sides and expanding the squared distance of the unconstrained step gives:

$$\|(x_t - u) - \eta g_t\|^2 = \|x_t - u\|^2 - 2\eta \langle g_t, x_t - u \rangle + \eta^2 \|g_t\|^2.$$

The three-term structure is revealing: the middle term can be negative (if the gradient aligns with the direction toward $u$), which shrinks the distance. The final term is always non-negative — a tax on moving that cannot be avoided. This tension between alignment and noise drives the entire regret analysis.

lemma ogd_step_expansion_squared_distance (P : Projection E) (η : ℝ) (x g u : E)
    (hu : ogd_projection_step_feasibility P u) :
    dist (ogd_update_rule_def P η x g) u ^ 2
      ≤ dist x u ^ 2 - 2 * η * ⟪g, x - u⟫ + η ^ 2 * ‖g‖ ^ 2 := by
  -- Apply Nonexpansiveness
  have h_nonexp := P.nonexpansive.dist_le_mul (x - η • g) u
  -- Clean up projection and constant
  rw [hu] at h_nonexp
  replace h_nonexp : dist (ogd_update_rule_def P η x g) u ≤ dist (x - η • g) u := by
    simpa using h_nonexp
  -- Square both sides
  have h_sq : dist (ogd_update_rule_def P η x g) u ^ 2 ≤ dist (x - η • g) u ^ 2 := by
    gcongr
  -- Move from 'dist' to 'norm'
  rw [dist_eq_norm, dist_eq_norm] at h_sq ⊢
  -- Expand quadratic form
  refine h_sq.trans ?_
  rw [sub_right_comm, norm_sub_sq_real]
  -- Simplify algebra
  simp only [inner_smul_right, norm_smul, Real.norm_eq_abs]
  -- η² identity
  have h_eta_sq : (|η| * ‖g‖) ^ 2 = η ^ 2 * ‖g‖ ^ 2 := by
    rw [mul_pow, sq_abs]
  rw [h_eta_sq, real_inner_comm]
  ring_nf
  apply le_refl
Lemma: Non-Expansiveness of Convex Projection

Claim:

$$\text{dist}(\Pi_{\mathcal{K}}(y), \Pi_{\mathcal{K}}(u)) \le \text{dist}(y, u)$$

Notation:

  • $\Pi_{\mathcal{K}}$ : The projection operator onto the closed convex set $\mathcal{K}$.
  • $y$ : The point in space being mapped into the set.
  • $u$ : A target point, typically the optimum.

Goal:

Projection onto a closed convex set is a contraction. This ensures that the projection step in the OGD update never pushes us further away from the optimal point $u$.

Full Narrative

A projection $\Pi_{\mathcal{K}}(v) = \arg\min_{w \in \mathcal{K}} \|w - v\|$ maps any point to its nearest neighbor in $\mathcal{K}$. The non-expansiveness property states that this map is a contraction: it never increases distances. Formally, $\|\Pi_{\mathcal{K}}(y) - \Pi_{\mathcal{K}}(u)\| \le \|y - u\|$.

This is critical for OGD because the update $x_{t+1} = \Pi_{\mathcal{K}}(x_t - \eta g_t)$ involves a projection. Without non-expansiveness, the projection could increase the distance to $u$, potentially unraveling the entire distance-based argument. With it, we know that projection can only help (or at least not hurt) convergence.

In Lean, this property is provided by mathlib's `Projection` structure, which bundles the projection operator with its proof of non-expansiveness. The lemma above is essentially a wrapper that applies this theorem to the specific arguments in the OGD update.

Lemma: Step-wise Regret Upper Bound

Claim:

$$f(x) - f(u) \le ⟪g, x - u⟫$$

Notation:

  • $f(x) - f(u)$ : The instantaneous regret at round $t$.
  • $⟪g, x - u⟫$ : The linearized surrogate for the objective gap.

Goal:

We cannot see the "optimal" loss $f(u)$ directly. This lemma shows that the alignment between the gradient and our error vector $(x-u)$ provides a surrogate measure for our loss.

Full Narrative

This lemma bridges the objective gap (what we care about) with an inner product (what we can algebraically manipulate). Starting from the first-order convexity inequality (Lemma 1) with $y = u$, we have:

$$f(u) \ge f(x) + \langle g, u - x \rangle.$$

Rearranging: $f(x) - f(u) \le \langle g, x - u \rangle$. The inner product $\langle g, x - u \rangle$ measures how much the gradient at $x$ "points toward" $u$. If $g$ is perfectly anti-aligned with $x - u$ (i.e., the gradient points directly toward the optimum), this inner product is negative, and the instantaneous regret is bounded by a negative number — the learner is better than the comparator at this round.

This is the "linearization reduction" from §7.4 of the theory: convexity lets us replace each $\ell_t(w)$ with its tangent-plane lower bound $\tilde{\ell}_t(w) = \ell_t(w_t) + \langle g_t, w - w_t \rangle$, reducing general convex losses to linear ones. The total regret on the original losses is bounded by the regret on these linear surrogates.

lemma step_regret_convexity_upper_bound (f : E → ℝ) (g x u : E)
    (hconv : convex_function_first_order_inequality f g x) :
    f x - f u ≤ ⟪g, x - u⟫ := by
  -- Apply convexity at u
  have h := hconv u
  -- Rearrange inequality
  have h' : f x - f u ≤ -⟪g, u - x⟫ := by linarith
  -- Use inner product properties to swap arguments
  have hx : ⟪g, x - u⟫ = -⟪g, u - x⟫ := by rw [← inner_neg_right, neg_sub]
  linarith [hx]
Lemma: Inner Product to Distance Progress Relation

Claim:

$$2 * \eta * ⟪g, x - u⟫ \le \text{dist}(x, u)^2 - \text{dist}(x_{t+1}, u)^2 + \eta^2 \|g\|^2$$

Notation:

  • $\text{dist}(x, u)^2 - \text{dist}(x_{t+1}, u)^2$ : The squared distance progress toward the optimum.
  • $\eta^2 \|g\|^2$ : The step-size penalty or noise term introduced by discretization.

Goal:

Rearranging Lemma 4 to isolate the inner product term. This establishes that progress toward the optimum is a "proxy" for low instantaneous regret.

Full Narrative

Lemma 4 gave us an upper bound on $\text{dist}(x_{t+1}, u)^2$. To connect it with regret, we need to isolate the inner product term $\langle g, x - u \rangle$ that appeared in Lemma 6. Rearranging the squared distance expansion:

$$2\eta \langle g, x - u \rangle \le \text{dist}(x, u)^2 - \text{dist}(x_{t+1}, u)^2 + \eta^2 \|g\|^2.$$

This is a purely algebraic consequence of Lemma 4 — it follows directly by moving terms with `linarith` in Lean. Yet it represents a profound conceptual shift: the inner product (which bounds regret) is now expressed as a combination of distance progress and noise.

The structure foreshadows the final regret bound: after summing over $T$ rounds, the distance terms telescope (leaving only the initial $\text{dist}(x_0, u)^2$), and the noise terms accumulate to $T \cdot \eta^2 L^2$. The optimal step size $\eta$ will balance these two contributions.

Lemma: Single-Step Regret Decomposition

Claim:

$$f(x) - f(u) \le \frac{\text{dist}(x, u)^2 - \text{dist}(x_{t+1}, u)^2 + \eta^2 \|g\|^2}{2\eta}$$

Notation:

  • $\frac{1}{2\eta}$ : The normalization factor required to convert distance into function value units.

Goal:

We express round $t$ regret as a sum of progress (change in distance) and noise.

Full Narrative

This lemma is the culmination of the single-step analysis. It chains together Lemmas 6 and 7:

  1. From Lemma 6: $f(x) - f(u) \le \langle g, x - u \rangle$ (convexity bounds regret by inner product).
  2. From Lemma 7: $2\eta \langle g, x - u \rangle \le \text{dist}(x, u)^2 - \text{dist}(x_{t+1}, u)^2 + \eta^2 \|g\|^2$ (inner product bounded by distance progress).
  3. Dividing the second inequality by $2\eta > 0$ and combining gives the result.

The final inequality $f(x) - f(u) \le (\text{progress} + \text{noise}) / (2\eta)$ has a beautiful interpretation: the instantaneous regret at round $t$ is at most the distance shrinkage toward $u$ plus a discretization penalty, all scaled by $1/(2\eta)$. If the learner suffers high regret at round $t$, they must have made proportionally large progress toward the optimal comparator $u$ — a fundamental fairness property of OGD.

The division by $2\eta$ is valid only because $\eta > 0$, which is ensured by the positivity condition $h\eta\_pos$ in the Lean statement.

Lemma: Optimal Step Size Balancing

Claim:

$$\frac{D^2}{2\eta} + \frac{\eta L^2 T}{2} = DL\sqrt{T} \text{ where } \eta = \frac{D}{L\sqrt{T}}$$

Notation:

  • $D$ : The diameter or maximum distance from $x_0$ to $u$.
  • $L$ : The Lipschitz constant bounding gradient norms.
  • $T$ : The time horizon or total number of steps.

Goal:

Solve for the $\eta$ that minimizes the aggregate bound $\frac{D^2}{2\eta} + \frac{\eta L^2 T}{2}$. Setting $\eta = \frac{D}{L\sqrt{T}}$ balances the initial error and cumulative noise perfectly.

Full Narrative

After summing the per-step bound from Lemma 8 over $T$ rounds, the regret is bounded by two competing terms:

$$\text{Regret}_T \le \frac{\|x_0 - u\|^2}{2\eta} + \frac{\eta}{2} \sum_{t=1}^T \|g_t\|^2 \le \frac{D^2}{2\eta} + \frac{\eta L^2 T}{2}.$$

The first term $\frac{D^2}{2\eta}$ grows as $\eta$ decreases (small steps mean slow initial progress). The second term $\frac{\eta L^2 T}{2}$ grows as $\eta$ increases (large steps mean more noise). The optimal $\eta$ minimizes their sum — a classic bias-variance tradeoff.

Setting the derivative to zero: $-\frac{D^2}{2\eta^2} + \frac{L^2 T}{2} = 0$ gives $\eta = \frac{D}{L\sqrt{T}}$. Plugging this back in yields the clean final bound $\text{Regret}_T \le DL\sqrt{T}$.

This lemma is purely algebraic — the Lean proof uses `ring`, `positivity`, and field simplification lemmas — but it represents the conceptual climax of the analysis: the existence of a step size that makes the average regret vanish as $T \to \infty$.

    D^2 / (2 * η) + η * L^2 * T / 2 = D * L * Real.sqrt T := by
  intro η
  have h_sqrt : (Real.sqrt T) * (Real.sqrt T) = T := Real.mul_self_sqrt (by positivity)
  have h_L2_T : L^2 * T = (L * Real.sqrt T)^2 := by

      _ = L^2 * ((Real.sqrt T) * (Real.sqrt T)) := by rw [h_sqrt]
      _ = L^2 * (Real.sqrt T)^2 := by ring
      _ = (L * Real.sqrt T)^2 := by ring
  have hx_pos : 0 < L * Real.sqrt T := by positivity
  have hD_ne : D ≠ 0 := ne_of_gt hD
  have hx_ne : L * Real.sqrt T ≠ 0 := ne_of_gt hx_pos

    rw [mul_div_assoc', div_div_eq_mul_div]

    rw [h_L2_T]
    have : (D / (L * Real.sqrt T)) * (L * Real.sqrt T)^2 / 2 = D * (L * Real.sqrt T) * (L * Real.sqrt T) / (L * Real.sqrt T) / 2 := by ring

    _ = D * (L * Real.sqrt T) / 2 + D * (L * Real.sqrt T) / 2 := by rw [H1, H2]
    _ = D * L * Real.sqrt T := by ring
Theorem: The Final $O(\sqrt{T})$ Regret Bound

Claim:

$$\sum_{t=1}^T (f_t(x_t) - f_t(u)) \le DL\sqrt{T}$$

Notation:

  • $\sum (f_t(x_t) - f_t(u))$ : The cumulative regret accumulated over $T$ iterations.
  • $O(\sqrt{T})$ : The sublinear growth rate, indicating the average regret $\to 0$.

Goal:

Summing Lemma 8 over rounds. Distances telescope, leaving only initial error and noise. The average regret vanishes as $T \to \infty$. This proves OGD is a "no-regret" learner.

Full Narrative

The final theorem assembles all the pieces. Summing Lemma 8 over $t = 1$ to $T$:

$$\sum_{t=1}^T (f_t(x_t) - f_t(u)) \le \frac{1}{2\eta} \sum_{t=1}^T (\text{dist}(x_t, u)^2 - \text{dist}(x_{t+1}, u)^2) + \frac{\eta}{2} \sum_{t=1}^T \|g_t\|^2.$$

The first sum telescopes: all intermediate distance terms cancel, leaving only $\text{dist}(x_0, u)^2 - \text{dist}(x_{T+1}, u)^2 \le \text{dist}(x_0, u)^2$. The second sum, by the Lipschitz assumption, is at most $T L^2$.

Applying the bound $\text{dist}(x_0, u) \le D$ and substituting $\eta = D/(L\sqrt{T})$ gives:

$$\text{Regret}_T \le \frac{D^2}{2\eta} + \frac{\eta L^2 T}{2} = DL\sqrt{T}.$$

This is the celebrated $O(\sqrt{T})$ regret bound for OGD. The average regret $\text{Regret}_T / T \le DL / \sqrt{T} \to 0$ as $T \to \infty$ — the learner's per-round performance asymptotically matches the best fixed decision in hindsight, despite facing an adversarially chosen sequence of losses.

In Lean, the proof requires an inductive accumulation lemma (`ogd_regret_inductive_bound`) to handle the telescoping sum without explicit `Finset` summation tricks, as noted in the "Lessons Learned" section of the project plan. The final step invokes the optimal step-size lemma (Lemma 9) to collapse the algebraic expression to $DL\sqrt{T}$.

        gcongr

  have h_dist_sq : dist (x 0) u ^ 2 ≤ D ^ 2 := by
    have hD_pos : 0 ≤ D := by linarith
    gcongr

  -- Drop non-positive final trailing distance
  have h_dist_T_pos : 0 ≤ dist (x T) u ^ 2 := by positivity

  have h_num : dist (x 0) u ^ 2 - dist (x T) u ^ 2 + η ^ 2 * ∑ t ∈ Finset.range T, ‖g t‖ ^ 2 ≤ D ^ 2 + η ^ 2 * ((T:ℝ) * L ^ 2) := by
